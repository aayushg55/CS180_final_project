Project 1: Poor Man's Augmented Reality

In this project, I captured a video and inserted a synthetic object into the scene. The basic idea is to use 2D points in the image whose 3D coordinates are known to calibrate the camera for every video frame and then use the camera projection matrix to project the 3D coordinates of a cube onto the image.

Setup:

I first drew a regular pattern on a rectangular box to get a set of known 3D points. I labeled 67 points and chose them so that they were not all planar. I then labeled all of the keypoints on the image manually.

Input video

Labeled Keypoints

Keypoints in 3D space

Propogating Keypoints to other Frames

I created my own keypoint detector using the harris corner detector. 

Given keypoints in some frame i, to detect keypoints in frame i+1, I first detected corners using the harris corner detector, keeping only the 200 points with the highest response. Since the points do not move by a large amount in consecutive frames, I found the closest keypoint (in pixel space) to each of the new keypoints from the previous keypoints. I further thresholded the matches so that only matches with a small distance were kept. This detector critically depends on small motions in the video, and no spurious corners within the threshold radius. This procedure was done for all consecutive pairs of frames i, i+1 starting from frame 0. While the keypoints appeared to slightly wiggle over frames, they are relatively stable and do not drop out.

Tracked Keypoints

Calibrating the Camera

Now that I had the 2D image coordinates of marked points and their corresponding 3D coordinates, I had an overdetermined system of equations relating world coordinates to pixel coordinates. As such, I used least squares to solve for the projection matrix to project the 3D world coordinates to pixel space. This was done separately for each frame in the video, since the camera has a different pose in each frame.


Projecting a cube in the Scene

Bells and Whistles: Mesh

I also attempted to place an arbitrary mesh onto the scene using pyrender. To do this, I first needed to further decompose the camera projection matrix into camera intrinsics and extrinsics matrices, as they are required by the renderer. I first used cv2.calibrateCamera to get an approximate intrinsics matrix, and then solved for the extrinsics matrices with the fixed intrinsic matrix using cv2.solvePnP  as the results of cv2.calibrateCamera were unstable. Additionally, due to the way in which pyrender defines the axes compared to openCV, the y and z axes needed to be negated. Finally, the camera pose required by the renderer needed to be the inverse of the extrinsics matrix, effectively the camera to world matrix.

Project 2: Neural Algorithm for Artistic Style

In this project, I re-implemented Neural Algorithm for Artistic Style, described in Gatys et al. (2015). It leverages deep neural networks to combine the content of one image and the style of another to create a new artistic image. This is achieved by using a pre-trained CNN to compare the features of the generated image with the style and content images.

Approach

As suggested by the Gatys paper, I utilized the pre-trained VGG-19 network, which consists of 16 convolutional layers and 5 pooling layers trained on ImageNet. I changed the max pooling layers to average pooling to allow for a better flow of gradients.

VGG-19 Network

Style Transfer Process

The intuition of this process is that different layers of the network can act as filters to extract various levels of features, ranging from high to low-level features. First, the content and style images are passed through the network to generate their responses at each level of the network. To get a mixture of the style and content information, the goal is for the generated image to match the responses of both the style and content images at different layers of the network. In particular, style is matched at the [conv1_1, conv2_1, conv3_1, conv4_1, conv5_1] layers and content is matched at the [conv4_2] layer, as described in the paper. For content, the responses are directly matched, while for style, the Gram matrices of the responses are matched. The following loss is minimized by changing the weights of the generated image:

One interesting finding was that the larger the content/style images were used, the less the style transferred. No matter how large the style weight was set when using large images, the same amount of style seemed to transfer. While the image quality is significantly improved, the generated image has both low and high-level features from the content image and only captures some high level features of the style, like color and contrast. This might be due to how the Gram matrix is constructed and normalized. As such, I ended up using medium-resolution images (~640x480) when possible since they struck a good balance.
